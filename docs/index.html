Energy & Supply Chain (PIR 3)

Power and cooling constraints are critical vulnerabilities for data centers, and are amplified by the compute divide. As outlined in "Global Data Center Density and AI Compute Concentration: Trends and Geopolitical Implications," the growth in hyperscale data centers is driven by AI workloads, which demand massive power and space. This reliance on energy makes data centers susceptible to disruptions in power supply, whether from natural disasters, cyberattacks, or physical sabotage. The concentration of compute power in a few regions also creates a dependency on those locations for AI capabilities. The news about Finland's Vatajankoski and E-Heat plugging a containerized data center into a local heating network demonstrates ways to improve energy efficiency and reduce reliance on traditional cooling methods. However, these solutions may not be scalable to larger hyperscale facilities. The 'Compute Divide,' as mentioned in "Global Data Center Density and AI Compute Concentration," means that over 80% of nations have zero infrastructure for large-scale AI compute, creating a global imbalance and potential points of leverage for those who control the infrastructure. Securing water supply and substations near key facilities is paramount, but also extremely vulnerable to kinetic action.
